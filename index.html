Logical Towers

Logical towers is a multiplayer educational card game designed to teach Boolean logic through structured gameplay. Each player receives a hidden object and builds a five-level tower of attributes that must stay logically true. The challenge is to make the tower specific enough to prove the object, but not so obvious that other players guess it first.

Genre Educational / Deduction / Strategy
Players 2-4
Play Time 15-20 minutes
Core Idea Build, defend, and infer logical descriptions

Learning Goals and Intended Audience

Intended Audience

Logical Towers is designed for high school students in grades 9-12. It works well in computer science, math, ELA, gifted education, or interdisciplinary problem-solving classrooms because students interact with logic through concrete nouns and visible clues.

The game supports students who benefit from discussion, structured reasoning, and low-pressure opportunities to revise their thinking. Because the attributes come from a shared deck, the game reduces the ambiguity of fully open-ended responses.

Learning Goals

  • Boolean Logic Application: Practice constructing and interpreting logical expressions using AND, OR, and NOT.
  • Constraint-Based Reasoning: Iteratively refine solution spaces by applying logical rules and ensuring every attribute remains true for a single hidden object.
  • Categorization and Attribute Reasoning: Strengthen classification skills by describing and organizing objects through attributes.
  • Deductive Reasoning: Infer hidden objects from partial logical descriptions and incomplete information.
  • Social Deduction and Strategic Thinking: Develop social deduction and strategic thinking by evaluating opponents’ towers and deciding when to guess or challenge.
  • Argumentation Skills: Develop argumentation skills by defending claims and challenging weak evidence.
  • Metacognitive Reflection: Build metacognitive habits by revising flawed towers instead of treating mistakes as final.

Game Concept and Mechanics

Concept

Logical Towers is a multiplayer deduction card game where players construct logical “towers” to describe a hidden object. The goal is to balance precision and ambiguity—revealing enough to validate correctness while avoiding early identification.

Gameplay

Each player is assigned a hidden noun card and aims to construct a public logical description of their object using attribute cards. Players begin with four attributes, with the remaining cards forming a draw and discard pile. On each turn, a player draws one card and may choose one action out of building their tower, demolishing it, replacing attribute cards, or guessing another player’s object. Any number of cards from their hand can be replaced in a round, allowing for strategic resource management.

Players build a five-level logical tower, where each level consists of either a single attribute or two attributes connected with an OR. Levels are connected using AND, resulting in net four AND connections across the tower. Players may use up to one OR and two NOT operators per tower, though their use is optional. OR allows players to introduce ambiguity while maintaining correctness, and NOT enables exclusion of properties. The final tower must be logically consistent and accurately describe the hidden object. Sample: Barrel can be described by wooden AND container AND ( heavy OR flat ) AND cylindrical AND NOT modern.

Players can dismantle their towers by removing levels one per round in a top-down, stack-like manner, supporting iterative refinement. At any point, a player may guess another player’s object; correct guesses eliminate the opponent, while incorrect guesses incur a penalty. The first player to complete a valid five-level tower and verify it against their object wins the game.

Core Mechanics

  • Preparation: Players are assigned hidden noun cards and dealt attribute cards from a shuffled deck, introducing randomness and initial conditions.
  • Hidden Information System: Each player is assigned a secret noun card, creating asymmetric information and driving deduction-based gameplay.
  • Resource Management: Players manage a limited hand of attribute cards drawn from a shared deck, making decisions about when to keep, discard, or replace cards.
  • Turn-Based Actions: On each turn, players draw a card and choose to build their tower, dismantle it, discard cards, or attempt to guess another player’s object.
  • Logical Construction: Players construct a five-level tower using attributes connected primarily by AND, with optional use of one OR (within the same category) and up to two NOT operators.
  • Rule Constraints: Towers must remain logically consistent, with no contradictions, and must accurately describe the player’s hidden object upon completion.
  • Validation: Players may guess opponents’ objects at any time; correct guesses eliminate the opponent, while incorrect guesses result in a penalty.

Interaction, Rules, and Feedback

Players interact with the game by building public evidence, interpreting other players' towers, challenging false claims, and making guesses. Challenges and guesses create immediate feedback: a correct guess disqualifies the target, a failed guess costs the player a turn, and a correct challenge disqualifies a player whose logic is false.

Progression comes from narrowing the set of possible objects over time. A player wins by completing a valid five-level tower, revealing the hidden object, and passing group validation.

Digital Version Plan

The paper prototype can be developed into a digital version by automating setup, tracking modifier limits, validating logical consistency, and storing a larger set of noun and attribute cards. A digital version can also provide instant rule feedback and teacher-facing analytics about common mistakes or guessing patterns. Learning Theories and Perspectives

Constructivism

Students build understanding by actively creating a logical model instead of memorizing terms in isolation.

Game example: Each player constructs and revises a five-level expression for a hidden object.

Behaviorism

The game uses immediate consequences to reinforce accurate reasoning and discourage careless play.

Game example: Failed guesses cost a turn, and successful challenges remove invalid reasoning from play.

Cognitive Learning

Players actively construct and refine internal mental models of logical relationships through gameplay, integrating new information with prior understanding.

Game example: Cognitive processes such as attention, memory, and deductive reasoning are required from players to interpret, evaluate, and reorganize information.

Situated Learning

Students use logic inside a meaningful activity where formal reasoning has a clear purpose.

Game example: Players use operators and attributes to protect their object and interpret public evidence.

Social Learning

Students learn through observing peers, comparing strategies, and discussing whether claims are justified.

Game example: Guessing and challenge mechanics keep all players engaged with each other's reasoning.

Metacognition

Strong play requires students to monitor whether a tower is both true and strategically useful.

Game example: The modify action encourages players to inspect and repair weak logic.

Findings from Playtesting

In-Class Playtesting on April 13th, 2026

Session Overview

This round of playtesting took place during Educational Game Design on April 13th, 2026. The session materials were prepared by Aparna Manoj, Ashlyn McClendon, Celeste Mason, and David Peeler. A paper prototype was used in class with two playsets of noun cards, a shared attribute deck grouped by category, logical operators, and a one-page rule sheet.

The class prototype used noun cards such as Bicycle, Book, Laptop, and Scissors, along with attribute cards such as metal, portable, electronic, foldable, paper, and has moving parts.

Procedure

Playtesting consisted of two sessions with four participants in each group. Players were given minimal instructions, read the rule sheet, and then began playing a round. During play, they drew cards, built towers, discussed possible strategies, and occasionally paused to ask clarifying questions. After the round, participants answered follow-up questions and completed a short questionnaire on their laptops or phones using team-assigned participant codes.

Observations and Feedback

General gameplay moved smoothly, and most groups transitioned between turns without much trouble. The most common pauses happened when players were deciding how to use attributes or when they were unsure about the exact use of OR and NOT. In one case, a player tried turning an attribute card upside down to show that it meant NOT, which suggested that the negation mechanic still needs clearer representation.

Players were actively talking through strategy, challenging one another, and laughing as the round progressed. One player said, "This is fun." Another player talked through confusion about stacking, grouping, and order of operations, which confirmed that the core idea was engaging but some parts of the rule system were still unclear during live play.

Survey Results and Main Concerns

  • Most players rated enjoyment at 3 or 4 out of 5.
  • Playability was rated very highly, with all players giving it 5 out of 5.
  • Reported learning gains ranged from 1 to 3 out of 5, although one participant noted that the target age group would likely benefit more strongly.
  • The biggest concerns were rule clarity, sequencing of actions, balancing, and a possible "Player 1 Advantage."
  • Players suggested adding a shared word bank to narrow the set of possible objects and make deductions easier to interpret.
  • One player felt that immediate disqualification after a successful challenge was too severe and suggested rebuilding instead.
  • Another player recommended adding an example tower to the instructions that shows how each operator works.
  • A further suggestion was to experiment with a hot/cold style hint system for guesses.

Interpretation

The responses suggest that the game concept is strong and socially engaging, but the rules need refinement so that operator use, grouping, and challenge timing are more intuitive. Written survey responses were generally positive about engagement, while more neutral on learning and challenge, which makes sense because the participants were not the exact target audience. The team also noted that the classroom setting and the fact that many participants already knew each other may have influenced the results.

Next Steps

Based on this session, the next iteration should focus on clearer instructions, stronger examples, attribute balance, difficulty scaling, and revisions to the guessing and challenge systems. The team also plans to keep refining the mostly linear tower structure because it is easier for players to read and simpler to imagine implementing in Unity than the earlier non-linear prototype.

  • Refine attribute balance.
  • Improve difficulty scaling.
  • Rewrite game instructions for better clarity.
  • Build an electronic version of the game.
